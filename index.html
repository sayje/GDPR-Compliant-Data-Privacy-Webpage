Data Privacy: RA 10173, GDPR, Cookie Policy, and Consent Management

By Jyssah Mae Pablo, BSIT-311

Published: November 14, 2025

Data Privacy: RA 10173

The Philippines Republic Act No. 10173, also known as the Data Privacy Act of 2012, and its Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR), aim to protect individuals' personal information in both government and private sector systems. The law aims to protect personal data security while promoting transparency and trust in digital systems, ensuring privacy without jeopardizing the free flow of information necessary for innovation and growth. The National Privacy Commission (NPC) is designated as the primary regulatory authority in charge of ensuring the Act's implementation and compliance.

The Data Privacy Act applies to all types of personal data, whether recorded in paper or electronic form, and covers both automated and manual processing systems. It establishes the rights of data subjects, including the right to be informed, the right to access, the right to object, and the right to erasure or blocking of personal data. Organizations are required to implement appropriate security measures to protect personal data from unauthorized access, disclosure, alteration, or destruction. They must also appoint a Data Protection Officer (DPO) responsible for ensuring compliance with the Act and handling data privacy-related matters within the organization (Republic Act No. 10173 Philippines, n.d.).

General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) Compliance

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which went into effect on May 25, 2018, is the world's strictest security and privacy law. Its purpose was to provide individuals with control over their personal data by establishing standards for the collection and processing of personal information for both EU and non-EU residents (Team, 2025).

The GDPR defines several objects that handle, process, and secure data. The GDPR defines three types of data parties: data subjects, controllers, and processors. A "data subject" is someone whose information is collected. A "controller" is an organization that determines the conditions, purpose, and methods for processing the data subject's personal information. A "processor" is an organization that handles personal information on behalf of the controller. Controllers and processors may be located anywhere in the world, including the United States, according to GDPR regulations (What Is GDPR? - Meaning & Compliance Checklist | ProofPoint US, 2025).

Another key GDPR term to understand is what constitutes personal data. GDPR does not protect all data, only that which can be used to identify an individual. For example, age alone cannot be used to identify someone and is not covered by GDPR, whereas age and name data can identify an individual and are covered (What Is GDPR? - Meaning & Compliance Checklist | ProofPoint US, 2025).

Any website that attracts European visitors, regardless of location, must follow these guidelines, which ensure the protection of personal data and hold companies accountable for how they handle such information. GDPR replaces the previous Data Protection Directive, strengthening consumer privacy rights while also standardizing data protection across all EU member countries (Team, 2025).

Cookie Policy

Cookies are small text files sent to your browser by websites you visit. They assist the website in remembering information about your visit, which can make returning to the site easier and the site more useful to you. Similar technologies, such as unique identifiers for apps or devices, pixel tags, and local storage, can all perform the same function.

Cookies and other technologies used for functionality allow you to access features that are essential to a service. These cookies are used to enable and maintain Google services. Things considered fundamental to a service include remembering choices and preferences, such as your language preference; storing session-related information, such as the contents of a shopping cart; enabling features or performing tasks requested by you; and product optimizations that help maintain and improve that service. These cookies are required for the website to function and cannot be disabled in our systems. They are typically only set in response to actions taken by you, such as changing your privacy settings, logging in, or filling out forms. You can set your browser to block or alert you about these cookies, but some parts of the site will not then work. These cookies do not store any personally identifiable information (How Google Uses Cookies – Privacy & Terms – Google, n.d.).

Website cookies are an essential part of a website. hey improve the user experience while also collecting data about the user's behavior on the site. This information can then be used to create better content, personalized ads, and other features (Staff, 2025).

Consent Management

Consent management is the process of obtaining, recording, and managing user consent to collect and use their personal information. This includes informing users about the data being collected, how it will be used, and their rights in relation to it. The term refers to the entire consent lifecycle, including storage and capture, data access requests, and deletion.

Consent management was first introduced by the GDPR in 2018, when companies were required to obtain permission before collecting personal information. Since then, it has become an essential component of data privacy compliance, with laws such as the CCPA, Quebec Law 25, and PIPEDA all incorporating consent management in some form (Husain, 2025). User privacy and data governance is becoming increasingly regulated. GDPR, CPRA, LGPD, and other region-specific privacy regulations affect exactly how you must obtain user consent and how the data can be used (What Is Consent Management?, n.d.).
